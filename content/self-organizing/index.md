Three times I have watched a community find itself. A latent group, distributed and anonymous, coalesced around an unaddressed problem before any institution recognized it as one — and before any organization moved to solve it. Then I helped catalyze what it became.

I call this pattern self-organizing rather than collective action deliberately. Collective action implies an existing movement, an organization that convened the group, a prior social infrastructure. None of that was present here. In each case, the community did not exist as a community until the right signal named the problem — and then organized itself before anyone asked it to.

## The Recipe

Once is a story. Twice is a pattern. Three times is a framework worth naming.

1. A public signal names an unaddressed problem and surfaces a latent community
2. Anonymous, distributed self-organization produces immediate outputs faster than any institution could
3. Socioemotional bonds form during the urgent phase — these are the load-bearing structure, not just a byproduct
4. Catalyzation (not direction) transitions the self-organized effort into something durable
5. Formal outputs follow: a nonprofit, a peer-reviewed paper, a platform

The sequence matters. The bonds that form in step three are what make step four possible. You cannot engineer that from the top down. What you can do is recognize it when it happens and steward it carefully.

## CRHQ / CEDR (2017)

In late August 2017, Hurricane Harvey was hours from landfall near Houston. Anyone watching the forecasts could see that Dallas emergency systems were going to be overwhelmed — not if, when. There was no organization standing by to handle the overflow. There was no protocol. There was a gap, and the gap was visible to anyone paying attention.

I put out a tweet.

Within hours, hundreds of pseudonymous volunteers had self-organized in Slack. They came from nowhere in particular — tech workers, data people, concerned citizens who had seen the tweet or seen someone retweet it. Nobody appointed them. Nobody told them where to go. They found each other because the signal was specific enough to name the problem and broad enough to reach everyone who cared about it.

What followed was remarkable. The group jointly leveraged Google, Tableau, and ESRI tools at no cost, built a triage workflow from scratch, and began human-reviewing rescue requests submitted via Twitter — real people reading real distress signals, verifying them, routing vital information to volunteer disaster responders on the ground. Faster than any institution could have assembled a response team. The effort scaled through Hurricanes Irma and Maria, developed coordination relationships with NAPSG and Firemappers, and eventually pivoted to wildfire response. The socioemotional bonds formed under pressure held. CrowdRescue HQ formalized as CEDR Digital Corps, a 100% volunteer 501(c)(3). I serve as Chair of the CEDR Digital Corps Board of Directors.

Here is the early-mover claim worth making explicit: CRHQ was solving human-in-the-loop curation, trust calibration, and volunteer coordination at scale in 2017, before "participatory AI/ML" was a named research area. The coordination challenges the AI/ML field is actively working through now — volunteer fatigue, trust calibration, socioemotional infrastructure, distributed authority design — were empirically navigated first in a disaster response context. The lessons exist. They were derived under real conditions, not laboratory ones.

## ICML Position Paper (2024–25)

Seven years later, the same pattern ran in a completely different domain.

I published a tweet critiquing "AGI as the north-star goal of AI research." The critique was specific: the term AGI is doing ideological work while masquerading as a technical goal — presuming consensus where there is none, laundering contested values as engineering objectives, excluding the communities most affected from the conversation about what AI systems are for.

The tweet surfaced a latent community. About fifteen researchers across institutions — people thinking about adjacent problems, some of whom had never met — began self-organizing around a shared concern. No conference convened them. No funding agency organized them. They found each other because the signal named something they already knew was true but had not seen named publicly.

The result was a peer-reviewed ICML 2025 publication: Blili-Hamelin, Graziul et al. (2025), "Position: Stop treating 'AGI' as the north-star goal of AI research," arXiv:2502.03689. The paper identifies six specific failure modes in AGI discourse: the Illusion of Consensus, Supercharging Bad Science, Presuming Value-Neutrality, Goal Lottery, Generality Debt, and Normalized Exclusion. It is, in miniature, an academic community performing its own version of self-organization around a shared concern — anonymous in the early stages, distributed across institutions, accelerated by the urgency of wanting to say something before the discourse calcified further.

## IDEP (Synthesis)

The third case is different in origin but continuous in logic. CEDR and the ICML paper both began with a tweet that surfaced a latent community. IDEP begins with the question: what would it look like to build the infrastructure for this kind of self-organizing to be sustainable, community-controlled, and not crisis-dependent?

The Illinois Data Equity Project is the institutionalization of the recipe. Its ultimate purpose is to develop and propagate resources for communities to form data stewardship organizations that self-organize, self-govern, and self-determine how to advance their collective and individual data rights. Not waiting for a hurricane. Not waiting for a discourse failure to become urgent enough that fifteen researchers finally coordinate. Building the conditions so that the latent community can find itself before the crisis arrives.

More at [idep.org](https://idep.org).

---

This is a capability, not a coincidence. Understanding what connects, aligns, and motivates a latent group to find each other and commit to a shared goal is transferable. CEDR proved it in disaster response. The ICML paper proved it in academic governance critique. IDEP is how you build the infrastructure so this kind of self-organizing doesn't require a crisis to start.
