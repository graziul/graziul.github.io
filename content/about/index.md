## Christopher M. Graziul

I finished my undergraduate degrees in Physics and Mathematics at Virginia Tech in 2004. My original interest was fusion energy — not for its elegance as a physics problem, but because energy is the primary constraint on human flourishing at civilizational scale. That framing has never left me. It oriented me toward problems that are hard, slow, and genuinely important, and toward patience with the gap between a solvable problem and a solved one. The VT Physics Department recognized that work with its 2003 student award.

Arriving at the University of Chicago for my MA, I did what physicists do when they encounter a new problem space: I ran a breadth search. I audited across the social science departments looking for the discipline that was most structurally rigorous and most adaptable — the one that would let me analyze social systems the way physics analyzes physical ones. Sociology won. The discipline had the clearest frameworks for understanding institutions, power, and the emergent properties of large human systems. It also taught me something physics training tends to skip: technical capability without structural analysis doesn't solve problems; it creates new ones, usually for whoever was already worst positioned.

My dissertation and subsequent postdoctoral work at Brown University turned that combination toward urban history. Working with John Logan and Reynolds Farley through Brown's Population Studies and Training Center, I helped build the geocoding infrastructure that assigned spatial coordinates to full-count census records for 69 U.S. cities, spanning 1900 to 1940 — roughly 30 million records for 1930 alone. That project, published as Logan, Graziul & Frey (2018), was fundamentally a data infrastructure problem at scale: designing ETL workflows that could handle historical inconsistency, integrating legacy code, and developing integrity metrics to know when the process was trustworthy. It was also the first time I confronted a pattern that has defined every major project since: the tools we have were not built for the questions we're actually asking.

In 2017, during Hurricane Harvey, I watched a coordination problem unfold in real time on social media. People in floodwater were posting their locations. Volunteers who could help couldn't find them. The information existed; the infrastructure to use it didn't. I sent a tweet. Within 48 hours, CrowdRescue HQ (CRHQ) had become a functioning operation — hundreds of volunteers, structured task allocation, real-time coordination across platforms. What I learned from that experience, and from the years of building that became CEDR Digital Corps, was not primarily about disaster response. It was about the sociology of online collaboration. Participatory online communities can do extraordinary things, but only when the socioemotional infrastructure is load-bearing — when trust, recognition, and conflict resolution are treated as first-order design problems, not nice-to-haves. That is the proof of concept for everything that followed.

Beginning in 2018 at the University of Chicago, I led the first systematic research program treating police radio communications as a dataset for understanding police behavior. The data was legally public — broadcast on radio frequencies anyone can receive. But no governance framework existed for research use of material that was public in legal status while being privacy-sensitive in practice. I built one. Working collaboratively with the UChicago Social and Behavioral Sciences IRB, the University Research Administration, and the Office of Legal Counsel, my team developed data use agreements, a DUA workflow, and a structured repository that allowed rigorous research while managing the genuine privacy and civil liberties concerns the data raised. That framework became the institutional model for the field. It also exposed the limits of what institutions will do on their own initiative when the costs of inaction fall on someone else.

That recognition led to the public advocacy phase that began around 2022. I started making the case in forums, papers, and direct engagement with policymakers and platform operators: if you want to govern AI systems responsibly, you have to govern the data ecosystems those systems depend on, including data that exists in the ambiguous zone between legally public and practically sensitive. Institutions will not do this voluntarily at the pace the technology requires. The argument requires external pressure, public frameworks, and demonstrated alternatives.

The current synthesis is two parallel structures. The Illinois Data Equity Project — IDEP, at [idep.org](https://idep.org) — is the platform: a community of practice and a set of tools for responsible data governance built around the communities most affected by data-driven systems. Graziul Advisory, at [graziul.io](https://graziul.io), is the practice: AI governance consulting for organizations navigating the governance gap between what the technology can do and what the law and ethics currently require.

### Not a unicorn

I am not a unicorn. I am a human who made deliberate choices at every stage for normative reasons. The combination of physics, sociology, GIS, and community organizing isn't magic — it's the result of asking at every stage: what do I need to understand this problem properly? Physics gave me comfort with quantitative rigor and tolerance for slow, hard problems. Sociology gave me the structural vocabulary to ask why institutions behave as they do. GIS and data engineering gave me the ability to build the infrastructure that research requires when it doesn't yet exist. And the work with CRHQ/CEDR gave me direct evidence that communities can govern themselves if the right scaffolding is present. Each choice was legible at the time. The combination is only unusual in retrospect.
